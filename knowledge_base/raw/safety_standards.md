# Industrial Process-Safety Standards Reference

Synthetic reference compiled for offline retrieval. Section numbers illustrate the
structure of OISD-STD-105, the Factory Act 1948, and DGMS technical circulars; treat
the exact numbers as indicative, not verbatim statutory text.

## Permit to Work System (OISD-STD-105 Sec 4.1 | Factory Act 1948 Sec 7A)
The Permit to Work (PTW) system controls all non-routine and hazardous work. No work in a
hazardous area may begin without a valid, signed permit that records the isolation, gas
test results, PPE, and the responsible persons. Permits are time-bound and must be
revalidated at every shift changeover. A permit must be suspended or cancelled the moment
the underlying conditions change — for example a rising gas trend, a failed gas test, loss
of ventilation, or an adjacent emergency. Simultaneous permits in the same or adjacent
zones must be cross-checked for conflicts before both are authorised.

## Hot Work (OISD-STD-105 Sec 8.1 | Factory Act 1948 Sec 37 | DGMS Circular 05)
Hot work includes welding, cutting, grinding, and any spark- or flame-producing activity.
A hot work permit requires a combustible-gas test below 10% of the Lower Explosive Limit,
removal of ignition sources, a trained fire watch, and continuous gas monitoring. The hot
work permit must be cancelled immediately if gas concentration exceeds 50 ppm, if the fire
watch leaves, or if flammable material enters the area. Hot work adjacent to a zone with
elevated gas is prohibited — combustible gas can migrate. This co-occurrence of hot work
and gas accumulation is the leading precursor to fatal explosions.

## Confined Space Entry (OISD-STD-105 Sec 7.4 | Factory Act 1948 Sec 36 | DGMS Circular 08)
A confined space has limited entry/exit and is not designed for continuous occupancy:
vessels, tanks, pits, sewers, silos. Entry requires a dedicated confined space permit,
atmospheric testing for oxygen, toxic gas, and flammability, continuous ventilation, and a
standby attendant with rescue capability. Entry is strictly prohibited without a standby
rescue team present. Oxygen must be between 19.5% and 23.5%. Never enter to attempt a
rescue without breathing apparatus — most confined-space fatalities are would-be rescuers.
Re-test the atmosphere after any break and after rainwater or process ingress.

## Gas Monitoring and Toxic Thresholds (OISD-STD-105 Sec 5.2 | DGMS Circular 05)
Continuous multi-gas monitoring is mandatory in hazardous zones. For hydrogen sulphide
(H2S): the 8-hour permissible exposure limit is 10 ppm, the short-term (15-minute) limit
is 15 ppm, and 100 ppm is Immediately Dangerous to Life or Health (IDLH). Gas above 50 ppm
requires increased ventilation and continuous monitoring; above 100 ppm requires immediate
evacuation and isolation of the source. Combustible gas at or above 10% of the Lower
Explosive Limit prohibits all hot work. Gas detectors alarm on their own threshold and
cannot see permit status, confinement, or a rising trend — which is why compound analysis
is required.

## Oxygen Deficiency and Enrichment (OISD-STD-105 Sec 6.1 | Factory Act 1948 Sec 36)
Normal oxygen is 20.9%. Below 19.5% is oxygen-deficient and unsafe for entry; below 18.0%
is immediately dangerous to life and health and requires evacuation; below 16% causes rapid
impaired judgement and unconsciousness. Above 23.5% is oxygen-enriched and dramatically
increases fire risk. Elevated combustible or inert gas can displace oxygen; a combined
reading of rising gas and falling oxygen indicates displacement and demands immediate
evacuation and ventilation.

## Compound Risk Conditions (OISD-STD-105 Sec 5.3)
The most dangerous conditions are combinations no single sensor flags: hot work with
sub-alarm gas accumulation in a confined space; maintenance activity that disturbs trapped
gas; a permit that stays active across a shift changeover supervision gap. The Visakhapatnam
Steel Plant coke-oven incident of January 2025 followed exactly this pattern — entrapped
gases during maintenance triggered a sudden explosion despite functioning individual
detectors. Compound risk scoring correlates gas, oxygen, permits, confinement, maintenance,
and shift timing to detect these combinations hours before any single alarm would fire.

## Lockout-Tagout and Isolation (OISD-STD-105 Sec 9.1 | Factory Act 1948 Sec 21)
Before any intrusive maintenance, all energy sources — electrical, mechanical, hydraulic,
pneumatic, thermal — must be isolated, locked, and tagged. Each worker applies a personal
lock. Verify zero energy before work. Electrical work in humidity above 85% requires ELCB
protection and insulation verification.

## Personal Protective Equipment (OISD-STD-105 Sec 3.4 | Factory Act 1948 Sec 41)
Minimum PPE in process areas: helmet, safety shoes, and eye protection. Task-specific PPE:
flame-retardant clothing for hot work, self-contained breathing apparatus (SCBA) for IDLH
or oxygen-deficient atmospheres, gas monitors for confined entry, and full-body harness for
work at height. PPE is the last line of defence, never a substitute for eliminating the
hazard.

## Emergency Response and Evacuation (OISD-STD-105 Sec 12.0 | Factory Act 1948 Sec 38)
On any STOP WORK or evacuation trigger, workers move to the designated muster point via the
nearest safe egress, upwind of any release. The area authority accounts for all personnel
using the permit register. The first ten minutes are decisive: raise the alarm, isolate the
source, deploy the emergency response team, preserve sensor evidence, and begin a
regulatory-compliant incident record. Multilingual alerts ensure every worker understands
the instruction to evacuate.

## Shift Handover (OISD-STD-105 Sec 4.4 | DGMS Circular 08)
Shift changeovers at 06:00, 14:00, and 22:00 are documented supervision blind spots. All
active permits, ongoing work, abnormal readings, and gas trends must be formally handed over
to the incoming crew. Work that continues across a handover requires explicit re-authorisation
and confirmation that the incoming supervisor is aware of current conditions.

## Regulatory Roles (Factory Act 1948 Sec 7A | DGMS)
The occupier holds the general duty of ensuring health and safety. The safety officer
administers the PTW system and audits compliance against OISD, the Factory Act, and DGMS
circulars. Deviations must be flagged before statutory audits and closed through corrective
action workflows.
